Top 10 Legal Cases
  • 1. Keshavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973) - Basic Structure Doctrine ▼
    The Keshavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala case (1973) is a landmark decision by the Supreme Court of India that established the "basic structure doctrine" of the Constitution. It arose from a challenge to the Kerala government's land reform legislation, which sought to reduce the powers of religious institutions over land. The Court ruled that while Parliament has the power to amend the Constitution, it cannot alter its fundamental framework or basic structure, which includes principles such as the supremacy of the Constitution, the rule of law, and the separation of powers. This doctrine has since been pivotal in maintaining the balance between parliamentary sovereignty and constitutional supremacy in India.
  • 2. Vishaka v. State of Rajasthan (1997) - Sexual Harassment Guidelines ▼
    The Vishaka v. State of Rajasthan case (1997) is a landmark judgment by the Supreme Court of India that addressed the issue of sexual harassment of women at the workplace. The case arose after a social worker, Bhanwari Devi, was gang-raped while trying to protect a child marriage case. The Court recognized that the absence of clear laws on sexual harassment violated women's rights to equality and dignity under the Constitution. It laid down comprehensive guidelines to prevent and address sexual harassment in workplaces, emphasizing the need for mechanisms to ensure safe work environments for women. This judgment served as a critical step towards the eventual enactment of the Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal) Act in 2013, underscoring the importance of legal protections against such violations.
  • 3. Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978) - Fundamental Rights Expansion ▼
    The Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India case (1978) is a pivotal Supreme Court ruling that expanded the interpretation of the right to life and personal liberty under Article 21 of the Indian Constitution. Maneka Gandhi challenged the government's decision to impose a travel ban on her without providing any reasons, arguing that it violated her rights. The Court held that the procedure established by law must be fair, just, and reasonable, thereby linking the right to life with the principles of natural justice. This judgment not only broadened the scope of Article 21 but also emphasized that fundamental rights cannot be abrogated without due process, reinforcing the protection of individual freedoms against arbitrary state action. It marked a significant shift towards a more substantive interpretation of rights in India.
  • 4. Shah Bano Case (1985) - Muslim Womenâ€™s Maintenance Rights ▼
    The Shah Bano case (1985) is a landmark decision by the Supreme Court of India that addressed the rights of Muslim women to alimony after divorce. Shah Bano, a divorced Muslim woman, sought maintenance from her ex-husband under Section 125 of the Criminal Procedure Code. The Supreme Court ruled in her favor, stating that the right to maintenance is not limited by personal laws and must adhere to the principles of justice and equality under the Constitution. This judgment emphasized that all women, regardless of religion, are entitled to maintenance and highlighted the need for reform in personal laws to align them with constitutional rights. The ruling sparked significant political and social debates, ultimately leading to the enactment of the Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Divorce) Act, 1986, aimed at limiting alimony and altering the legal landscape for Muslim women in India.
  • 5. S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994) - Federalism and State Governments. ▼
    The S.R. Bommai v. Union of India case (1994) is a significant ruling by the Supreme Court of India that clarified the constitutional limits of the President's power under Article 356, which allows for the imposition of President's Rule in states. The Court held that this power should be exercised with caution and only under specific circumstances, emphasizing that the arbitrary dismissal of a state government undermines the democratic process. It established that the judiciary could review the proclamation of President's Rule and laid down guidelines to prevent its misuse, reinforcing the importance of federalism and the rights of states within the Indian Constitution. This case significantly strengthened the balance of power between the Centre and state governments.
  • 6. Olga Tellis v. Bombay Municipal Corporation (1985) - Right to Livelihood. ▼
    This case addressed the rights of pavement dwellers in Mumbai facing eviction due to municipal regulations. The Supreme Court ruled that the right to livelihood is an integral part of the right to life under Article 21 of the Constitution, emphasizing that urban poor have a right to be heard before being evicted, thereby upholding their dignity and access to sustenance.
  • 7. Indra Sawhney v. Union of India (1992) - Reservations and Affirmative Action. ▼
    This important ruling from the Supreme Court examined the reservation policy for OBCs in government jobs and educational institutions. The Court upheld the validity of reservations but limited them to 27% and introduced the "creamy layer" concept, distinguishing between economically backward classes and those who have benefited from affirmative action, thereby ensuring that reservations do not perpetuate socio-economic backwardness.
  • 8. Aruna Shanbaug Case (2011) - Right to Die with Dignity. ▼
    This case involved a nurse, Aruna Shanbaug, in a persistent vegetative state for decades after a brutal assault. The Supreme Court addressed the issue of euthanasia and passive mercy killing, ultimately ruling against allowing Arunaâ€™s euthanasia but acknowledging the right to die with dignity. The judgment also called for comprehensive legislation on euthanasia.
  • 9. Navtej Singh Johar v. Union of India (2018) - Decriminalization of Homosexuality. ▼
    This landmark ruling decriminalized consensual same-sex relations by striking down Section 377 of the Indian Penal Code, which criminalized homosexual acts. The Supreme Court emphasized the importance of individual dignity, personal autonomy, and equality, marking a significant step towards LGBTQ+ rights and affirming that love and sexual orientation are intrinsic aspects of human identity.
  • 10. Ayodhya Verdict (2019) - Landmark Property Dispute Settlement. ▼
    The Supreme Court delivered a historic judgment on the long-standing Ayodhya dispute, concluding that the disputed land should be handed over to a trust for the construction of a Ram temple. The Court emphasized the need for maintaining communal harmony, evaluated historical claims, and recognized the importance of the site to Hindus, while also directing the allocation of alternative land for Muslim worship, thus attempting to address the complex socio-religious sentiments surrounding the issue.
